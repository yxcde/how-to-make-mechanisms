But first

I have an Idea but what do I do first?

Try deconstructing your idea - Does it need...

INPUT

 How do I and others communicate with my idea?
  • What kind of input?
  • Is it interactive, reactive or simply does it's own thing?

OUTPUT

How does my idea communicate with others?
  • Does it move?
  • Is it visual?
  • Does it have sound?

INPUT options

  • Sensors (light, sound, gas, etc.)
  • User input (Knobs, keyboard, body input such as Kinect)
  • Data from the internet or from other sources
  • Mixtures of all or some of the above?

OUTPUT options

  • Motors (Stepper, DC, Servo)
  • Lights (LEDs, DMX controlled stage lights)
  • Small speakers or more intricate sound design

Making a wishlist

Making a wishlist

  1. It needs to sense people and their movement
  2. It should make waves in water
  3. The waves should be translated into sound

Translating our wishlist

  1. sense people = some sensor input
  2. make waves = a kinetic system
  3. sound = a data-driven system of speakers

Divide and conquer - Julius Caesar

Use your wishlist to define parts that need to work

and go explore

Refine your wishlist into a functionality list

Functionality list for superpose

  • Sense change in distance to the table from an audience member = distance sensor
  • Create controlled waves silently = stepper motor with position control
  • Create sound from the patterns the wave create on the water

Build code in individual sketches

  1. Sensor control
  2. Motor control
  3. Communication of the sensor and the motor
  4. Calibration of input-output behaviour
  5. Start-up routine

We create our own toolbox to come back to

Sensor codes to build and try

changes in distance

  1. Get control of the sensor
    1. Get data
    2. Test sending or using data for something
    3. Maybe normalise data to a 0.0 - 1.0 range
    4. Add a self-calibration routine at power-on

Motor codes to build and try

controlled and silent moves

  1. Get control of the motor
    1. Make it move
    2. Test speed and maximum acceleration
    3. Find a stable way to send data to control movement
    4. Add a homing routine at power-on for position control (with a limit switch or other sensor)

Sound control

Translate patterns to sound

  1. Recognise patterns with image or sensor input
  2. If external sound synthesis (aka. Ableton or similar), a communication protocol is needed (i.e. MIDI or OSC)
  3. Simple MIDI control sketch for notes and volume change
  4. Data-driven changes

Start integrating your individual functionalities into a larger system

Ask yourself what is needed for the idea to work. Define input, output and behaviour. Sketch and try. Build in blocks that do one thing and keep those blocks to come back to. Build the larger systems from your blocks.

"Hardening" a prototype isn't about making it work – it's about making it last

Today's focus:

  • Enclosure and mounting
  • Mechanical parts
  • Secure wiring
  • Stable power supply
  • Reboot-proof code

Perma-Proto Boards

Soldering components

What to solder?

  • All passive parts (resistors, capacitors, LEDs, connectors)
  • Use pin headers and pin sockets for removable parts
  • Use cables and connectors for longer connections

Proto Shields

Cables

Do not solder jumper wires; they break easily.

Use solid-core or stranded-core cable instead.

Ribbon cables

Ribbon cables

  • Multiple connections in the same direction
  • Rainbow ones make it easier not to confuse end positions

Use connectors for parts that you need to unplug

Check that the ground is connected correctly!!!

Leave a gap between your PCB/soldered Proto-board and any enclosure. Electrically isolate it from any metal pieces or cases you might use. Use standoffs for this.

Mounting

3D Printing

  1. Higher than 50% infill usually won't offer better mechanical stability.

    -> Go for more (3+) perimeters (walls).
  2. If the part is small, try using a Brim to hold the corners down.

Tip:

  • If necessary: Increase mechanical strength by filling the inside of your print with resin.

    -> Pay attention to the resins curing temperature!

If you print a case, ensure a hole or button to reach the reset button on your Microcontroller/Arduino.

Test your build continuously for more than one day. Software and Hardware

What to look for in testing:

  • Is the temperature ok?
  • What is the ambient temperature while you test?
  • Are the mechanics still working?
  • Is something making scratching or squeaking sounds?

Movement and mechanics need lubrication. Use mechanical grease where possible or lubrication sprays for smaller parts.

For your computer:

  • Ensure you have remote access if you plan to exhibit it in a venue where you will not be physically present.
  • Deactivate all auto-update functions.
  • Reboot the computer on a timer (maybe once a week)

DOs & DON'Ts

DOs

  • Use soldered perma-proto boards or custom PCBs to replace breadboards.
  • Apply shrink tubing to secure wire joints.
  • Use screw terminals or pluggable connectors (JST, Molex) for replaceable components.
  • Add capacitors to stabilise power if motors or LEDs are involved.

DON’Ts

  • Don’t leave wires loose or tensioned—they’ll break or disconnect.
  • Don’t use breadboards alone for multi-day use or with moving parts.
  • Don’t mix power grounds (e.g., USB + external power) without care.

Electronics

DOs

  • Use thread-locking compounds (e.g., Loctite) or lock washers on moving assemblies.
  • Add mechanical stops to prevent motor overrun.
  • Use flexible couplings or rubber isolators to dampen vibrations.
  • Always plan for manual override or emergency shutoff for kinetic elements.

DON’Ts

  • Don’t over-tighten or glue servos and motors—leave room for serviceability.
  • Don’t mount moving parts directly to fragile structures.
  • Don’t ignore torque or weight mismatches.

Mechanics / Kinetics

DOs

  • Use brass heat inserts for reliable screw fittings.
  • Design modular parts for easy replacement or repair.
  • Print with PETG or PLA+ for strength and temperature stability.
  • Add ventilation slots if electronics generate heat.
  • Plan for access to reset buttons.

DON’Ts

  • Don’t rely on press-fit alone unless intentionally designed.
  • Avoid designs with too-thin walls or unsupported overhangs.
  • Don’t enclose electronics without airflow or access.

3D Printing and Cases

DOs

  • Utilise laser-cut acrylic, wood, or 3D-printed enclosures for protection and style.

    Mount enclosures using bolts, Velcro, magnets, or brackets, depending on the setup.
  • Leave cutouts for SD cards, USB ports, or status LEDs.

DON’Ts

  • Don’t hide controls or indicators needed during setup or failure!
  • Don’t assume everything will stay plugged in — account for human interaction (and stupidity).

Enclosures and Mounting

DOs

  • Include watchdog timers and restart mechanisms for long uptime.
  • Use non-blocking code (millis() instead of delay()).
  • Log errors (Serial or LED indicators).
  • Design for headless operation, aka plug-n-play (boots automatically, no user setup).

DON’Ts

  • Don’t rely on serial monitors or debugging tools at runtime.
  • Avoid making last-minute code changes unless they are well-tested.
  • Don’t overcomplicate the interface — make it idiot-proof.

Code and Software

Checklist for your prototype:

  • Enclosure and mounting secure
  • Mechanical parts well balanced
  • Secure wiring
  • Stable power supply
  • Reboot-proof code
